Alberta Biology 20 and Chemistry 30.

11 min estimatedLast updated July 15, 2026

Notes, flashcards, questions and exam preparation.

Chemistry 30 has a diploma examination. Biology 20 does not; Biology diploma preparation applies to Biology 30.

Biology 20

Unit A

Unit A: Energy and Matter Exchange in the Biosphere

  • Photosynthesis stores light energy in glucose; cellular respiration releases energy from glucose into ATP.
  • Energy enters most ecosystems as sunlight and flows one-way through trophic levels, while matter cycles.
  • Biogeochemical cycles include water, carbon, nitrogen and phosphorus.
  • Gross primary productivity is total photosynthetic production; net primary productivity subtracts plant respiration.
  • Ecological pyramids show energy, biomass or numbers. Energy transfer between trophic levels is inefficient.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Biosphere
All regions of Earth where life exists.
NPP
Energy stored by producers after subtracting respiration.
Biomagnification
Increasing concentration of a persistent substance at higher trophic levels.
Carbon sink
A reservoir that stores more carbon than it releases.
Chemosynthesis
Production of organic molecules using chemical rather than light energy.
1. Which process removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere most directly?
Answer: Photosynthesis
Photosynthesis converts carbon dioxide into organic molecules.
2. Why is less energy available at each higher trophic level?
Answer: Energy is lost as heat and through metabolism
Organisms use energy for life processes and release much of it as heat.
3. A lake receives excess fertilizer. Which change is most likely first?
Answer: Rapid algal growth
Nutrient enrichment can cause eutrophication and algal blooms.
Unit B

Unit B: Ecosystems and Population Change

  • Population size changes through births, deaths, immigration and emigration.
  • Exponential growth occurs under ideal conditions; logistic growth slows near carrying capacity.
  • Density-dependent factors include competition and disease; density-independent factors include floods and fires.
  • Natural selection changes allele frequencies when heritable traits affect reproductive success.
  • Succession describes predictable community change after disturbance.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Carrying capacity
Largest population an environment can support over time.
Biotic potential
Maximum reproductive capacity under ideal conditions.
Founder effect
Reduced genetic variation when a new population begins from few individuals.
Primary succession
Community development where soil is initially absent.
Limiting factor
A resource or condition restricting population growth.
1. Which factor is density dependent?
Answer: Disease transmission
Disease usually spreads more easily in dense populations.
2. What is the shape of logistic growth?
Answer: S-shaped
Growth slows as population approaches carrying capacity.
3. After lava creates bare rock, which process begins?
Answer: Primary succession
Primary succession begins where soil is absent.
Unit C

Unit C: Photosynthesis and Cellular Respiration

  • Light-dependent reactions occur in thylakoid membranes and produce ATP, NADPH and oxygen.
  • The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma and uses ATP and NADPH to build carbohydrates.
  • Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm; the Krebs cycle and electron transport chain occur in mitochondria.
  • Oxygen is the final electron acceptor in aerobic respiration.
  • Fermentation regenerates NAD+ when oxygen is unavailable but yields little ATP.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Thylakoid
Membrane structure where light-dependent reactions occur.
Stroma
Fluid region of chloroplast where the Calvin cycle occurs.
Glycolysis
Breakdown of glucose into pyruvate in the cytoplasm.
ATP synthase
Enzyme that uses a proton gradient to make ATP.
NADH
Electron carrier used in cellular respiration.
1. Where does glycolysis occur?
Answer: Cytoplasm
Glycolysis is a cytoplasmic pathway.
2. What is released when water is split during photosynthesis?
Answer: Oxygen
Photolysis of water releases oxygen.
3. Which stage produces most ATP in aerobic respiration?
Answer: Electron transport chain
Oxidative phosphorylation produces the greatest ATP yield.
Unit D

Unit D: Human Systems

  • Digestive organs mechanically and chemically break food into absorbable molecules.
  • Gas exchange depends on concentration gradients, thin membranes and large surface area.
  • The circulatory system transports gases, nutrients, wastes, hormones and heat.
  • Kidneys regulate water, ions, pH and nitrogenous waste through filtration, reabsorption and secretion.
  • Homeostasis uses negative feedback to maintain internal conditions.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Alveolus
Tiny air sac where gas exchange occurs.
Nephron
Functional unit of the kidney.
Peristalsis
Wave-like smooth muscle contractions moving material through the digestive tract.
Hemoglobin
Protein in red blood cells that binds oxygen.
Negative feedback
Response that reverses a deviation from a set point.
1. Where is most nutrient absorption completed?
Answer: Small intestine
The small intestine has villi and microvilli for absorption.
2. Which blood vessel carries oxygenated blood from lungs to heart?
Answer: Pulmonary vein
Pulmonary veins return oxygenated blood to the left atrium.
3. Which nephron process moves useful substances back into blood?
Answer: Reabsorption
Reabsorption returns water and solutes to the blood.

Chemistry 30

Unit A

Unit A: Thermochemical Changes

  • System and surroundings exchange energy as heat and work.
  • Exothermic reactions have negative enthalpy change; endothermic reactions have positive enthalpy change.
  • Calorimetry uses q = mcΔT for heat changes in a substance.
  • Hess's law adds reactions and enthalpy changes because enthalpy is a state function.
  • Standard molar enthalpy is the enthalpy change per mole for a specified process under standard conditions.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Enthalpy
Heat content of a system at constant pressure.
Exothermic
Process that transfers heat from system to surroundings.
Activation energy
Minimum energy needed for an effective collision.
Hess's law
Overall enthalpy equals the sum of step enthalpies.
Calorimeter
Device used to measure heat transfer.
1. A reaction warms its surroundings. It is most likely...
Answer: Exothermic
Heat is released to the surroundings.
2. If 100 g of water increases by 5.0°C, what expression gives q?
Answer: 100(4.19)(5.0)
Use q = mcΔT.
3. When a chemical equation is reversed, ΔH is...
Answer: Sign-reversed
Reversing the process reverses heat flow.
Unit B

Unit B: Electrochemical Changes

  • Oxidation is loss of electrons; reduction is gain of electrons.
  • Oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction at the cathode.
  • Spontaneous galvanic cells convert chemical energy to electrical energy.
  • Electrolytic cells require electrical energy to drive nonspontaneous reactions.
  • Standard cell potential is found from reduction potentials using E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Anode
Electrode where oxidation occurs.
Cathode
Electrode where reduction occurs.
Salt bridge
Completes the circuit and maintains electrical neutrality.
Oxidizing agent
Species that gains electrons and is reduced.
Electrolysis
Use of electricity to drive a nonspontaneous reaction.
1. Where does reduction occur?
Answer: Cathode
Reduction always occurs at the cathode.
2. In a galvanic cell, electrons flow...
Answer: From anode to cathode
Electrons are released by oxidation at the anode.
3. A positive standard cell potential indicates the forward reaction is...
Answer: Spontaneous under standard conditions
Positive E°cell corresponds to negative ΔG°.
Unit C

Unit C: Chemical Changes of Organic Compounds

  • Organic nomenclature identifies the longest carbon chain and functional groups.
  • Important functional groups include alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters and amines.
  • Addition reactions commonly occur at carbon-carbon multiple bonds.
  • Esterification combines a carboxylic acid and alcohol to form an ester and water.
  • Polymers form from repeating monomers through addition or condensation reactions.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Homologous series
Family of compounds with the same functional group and general pattern.
Isomer
Compound with the same molecular formula but different structure.
Esterification
Reaction of an alcohol and carboxylic acid to form an ester.
Monomer
Small molecule used to build a polymer.
Functional group
Reactive atom or group determining characteristic reactions.
1. Which pair can form an ester?
Answer: Alcohol and carboxylic acid
Esterification combines an alcohol and carboxylic acid.
2. Compounds with the same formula but different structures are...
Answer: Isomers
Structural isomers share a molecular formula.
3. Ethene reacting with bromine is an example of...
Answer: Addition
Atoms add across the double bond.
Unit D

Unit D: Chemical Equilibrium Focusing on Acid-Base Systems

  • Dynamic equilibrium occurs when forward and reverse reaction rates are equal.
  • Le Châtelier's principle predicts how equilibrium shifts after a stress.
  • Equilibrium constants compare product and reactant concentrations at equilibrium.
  • Brønsted-Lowry acids donate protons and bases accept protons.
  • Buffers contain a weak acid/base conjugate pair and resist sudden pH change.

Click a flashcard to reveal the answer.

Dynamic equilibrium
Forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates.
Ka
Acid ionization constant.
Conjugate base
Species remaining after an acid donates H+.
Buffer
Solution that resists pH change.
Equivalence point
Point where stoichiometric amounts have reacted in a titration.
1. Adding reactant to an equilibrium generally shifts it...
Answer: Toward products
The system consumes part of the added reactant.
2. A Brønsted-Lowry base is a...
Answer: Proton acceptor
Bases accept H+.
3. Which mixture can act as a buffer?
Answer: Weak acid and its conjugate base
A conjugate weak pair can neutralize added acid or base.

Exam Preparation

Biology 20

Unit & Final Exam Prep

Biology 20 is not a provincial diploma-exam course. These are original Alberta-style questions.

1. Why does a food web usually contain fewer organisms at high trophic levels?
Less usable energy is transferred upward.
Energy is lost through metabolism and heat.
2. Which structure gives the small intestine a large surface area?
Villi and microvilli
They increase absorptive area.
3. What happens to a population above carrying capacity?
Resources become limiting and mortality/emigration may rise.
Environmental resistance pushes the population downward.
4. Which molecule is the final electron acceptor in aerobic respiration?
Oxygen
Oxygen accepts electrons and combines with hydrogen to form water.
5. What is the primary role of hemoglobin?
Transport oxygen
Iron-containing heme groups bind oxygen reversibly.
6. Which process returns nitrogen gas to the atmosphere?
Denitrification
Denitrifying bacteria reduce nitrates to nitrogen gas.
7. Where does the Calvin cycle occur?
Chloroplast stroma
The light-independent reactions occur in the stroma.
8. What is a key function of the nephron loop?
Create a medullary concentration gradient
This allows urine concentration.
Chemistry 30

Diploma Prep

Original practice based on Alberta outcomes, not secured exam items.

1. A 50.0 g metal sample absorbs 1.20 kJ and warms by 30.0°C. What is its specific heat capacity?
0.800 J/(g·°C)
Use c=q/(mΔT)=1200/(50.0×30.0).
2. Which change increases the equilibrium yield of an exothermic product?
Lowering temperature
Removing heat favours the exothermic direction.
3. In a galvanic cell, which species is the strongest oxidizing agent?
The species with the most positive reduction potential
A stronger tendency to be reduced means stronger oxidizing ability.
4. What organic product forms from ethanol and ethanoic acid?
Ethyl ethanoate
Alcohol + carboxylic acid produces an ester and water.
5. If pH = 3.25, what is [H3O+]?
5.6 × 10^-4 mol/L
[H3O+]=10^-pH.
6. A catalyst is added to an equilibrium system. What changes?
Forward and reverse rates increase equally
A catalyst does not change equilibrium position or K.
7. Which electrode is negative in a galvanic cell?
Anode
Electrons are produced at the galvanic anode.
8. What is the conjugate acid of NH3?
NH4+
NH3 accepts H+.

The Thyroid Gland: Why One Small Organ Can Affect Your Entire Life

2 min estimatedLast updated July 15, 2026

How thyroid hormones influence energy, metabolism, concentration and mood, including hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, testing and treatment.

Educational purpose: General information only; not a diagnosis or substitute for medical care.
Approximately 2 min readLast updated July 15, 2026

Why this matters

In March 2025, MedMinute founder Saad Aboumalik was diagnosed with hypothyroidism after persistent fatigue, weakness, brain fog, low energy and reduced performance in school and sports. Reaching a diagnosis required learning which tests to request and which specialists manage thyroid disease. That experience helped inspire MedMinute: clear health education cannot diagnose disease, but it can help people recognize persistent changes and ask informed questions sooner.

Where the thyroid is located

The thyroid is a small, butterfly-shaped gland at the front of the neck, below the voice box and in front of the trachea. Its two lobes are joined by a narrow bridge of tissue called the isthmus.

How it works

The thyroid makes thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). The pituitary gland releases thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), which signals the thyroid to produce hormones. These hormones influence how cells use energy and therefore affect heart rate, temperature, digestion, muscle function, growth, concentration and mood.

Hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism

Hypothyroidism means the gland produces too little hormone, which can cause fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, slowed thinking and weight gain. Hyperthyroidism means excessive hormone production and may cause palpitations, heat intolerance, tremor, anxiety, sweating, sleep difficulty and weight loss.

Diagnosis

Testing usually begins with TSH and free T4. Free T3 is particularly helpful when hyperthyroidism is suspected. Thyroid antibody tests may support diagnoses such as Hashimoto’s disease or Graves’ disease. Ultrasound or nuclear medicine testing is used only when clinically appropriate.

Treatment

Hypothyroidism is commonly treated with levothyroxine, with the dose adjusted using repeat blood testing. Hyperthyroidism may be treated with antithyroid medication, radioactive iodine or surgery, depending on the cause and the patient. Thyroid disease is usually managed rather than described as having one universal cure.

MedMinute takeaway

Persistent, severe or worsening symptoms should be discussed with a qualified healthcare professional.
